# Movie Nights

Hacker Movie Nights: curated screenings of films, documentaries, and talks that shaped the culture — followed by unfiltered operator discussion. Hackers, Sneakers, The Social Network, Zero Days, DEFCON talks, Black Hat keynotes. We watch. We dissect. We extract the lessons Hollywood missed — and the ones they accidentally got right.

## Why Movie Nights

Technical skill isn't built only at a keyboard. It's built by studying history, psychology, strategy, and the human systems that technology lives inside. Film compresses decades into hours. It lets us witness decisions we haven't made yet. It lets us study the anatomy of failure and the architecture of breakthrough without paying the price ourselves.

We don't pick movies because they're popular. We pick them because they **teach**.

## The Format

### Screening (90–120 min)
- We watch together (Discord stream or in-person projector)
- No pausing. No commentary. Absorb the story.

### Dissection (45–60 min)
- We pause at key moments. We break down what just happened technically.
- Could the social engineering in a founder-origin story actually work?
- What would a real pentest of early-stage infrastructure look like?
- Is the malware in a hacker drama plausible?
- How would a real AI alignment problem manifest?
- We separate the real from the fictional. Then we ask: *what fiction has inspiration we can bring to reality?*

### Extraction (15 min)
- Each person writes one actionable takeaway: detection idea, defense strategy, mental model, tool concept
- Shared in `#movie-nights` channel. Indexed in the Refinery.

## Two Tracks

### Hacking Track
Technical cinema — documentaries and narratives revealing system architecture, attacker psychology, vulnerability consequences, nation-state operations, hacktivism, underground history.

**Examples:**
- *Zero Days* (Stuxnet) → Supply chain, air-gap jumping, nation-state OPSEC
- *The Great Hack* (Cambridge Analytica) → Data pipeline, psychographic targeting, platform abuse
- *DEFCON 27: Hacking Voting Machines* → Physical access, hardware implants, responsible disclosure
- *Black Hat 2021: PrintNightmare* → Print spooler, RCE chain, patch gaps

### Life-Themed Track
Films about the human condition under pressure. Whistleblowers, prosecutors, prodigies, communities. The cost of truth. The psychology of obsession. The architecture of resilience. These are not "break" films — they are the context that makes the technical work meaningful.

**Examples:**
- *The Social Network* → Scale, betrayal, technical debt, legal architecture
- *Snowden* → Compartmentalization, whistleblowing tradecraft, journalist OPSEC
- *Citizenfour* → Encryption in practice, meeting discipline, source protection
- *Steve Jobs* (2015) → Product vision, reality distortion, shipping vs perfecting

## Selection Criteria

| Criterion | Weight | Why |
|-----------|--------|-----|
| Historical significance | 25% | First depiction of X; defined the genre |
| Technical accuracy | 30% | Gets the details right (or instructively wrong) |
| Strategic depth | 25% | Shows *why*, not just *how* |
| Philosophical weight | 20% | Consequences, ethics, trade-offs |

We do not announce films in advance. The surprise is part of the discipline.

## What You Get Out of It

- **Mental models** — Patterns repeat. Stuxnet's USB propagation → NotPetya's MeDoc update → SolarWinds' build pipeline. Same shape.
- **Detection intuition** — "That lateral movement in the film? That's T1021.004. Here's the Sigma rule."
- **Strategic perspective** — The attacker's constraints. The defender's blind spots. The organizational failures that enabled both.
- **Community** — You watch with people who will pause and say "that's not how heap spraying works" and then explain how it actually works.

## For Visitors

You're welcome. No membership required. Just show up ready to engage. If you haven't seen the film before, you'll watch and listen — the dissection assumes familiarity. That's fine. You'll still learn.

Bring a notebook. The best insights come from the person next to you saying "wait, that's actually CVE-20XX-XXXX" and pulling up the advisory on their phone.

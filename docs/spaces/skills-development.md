# Skills Development

## Beyond Code

A Cybersecuritist who cannot cook is fragile. A Cybersecuritist who cannot speak is invisible. A Cybersecuritist who cannot manage money is a vulnerability.

We do not train technicians. We build exceptional humans who happen to be exceptional hackers.

## Cooking

**Why cooking?** Discipline. Precision. Self-sufficiency. Chemistry you can eat.

A Cybersecuritist understands systems. A kitchen is a system. Inputs (ingredients), process (recipe + technique), output (meal). Variables: heat, time, acid, fat, salt, Maillard reaction, emulsification, gelatinization.

We do not follow recipes. We understand them. Then we improvise.

**What we learn:**
- Knife skills — the debugger of the kitchen
- Mother sauces — the standard library of French cuisine
- Butchery — memory management for protein
- Fermentation — supply chain security for flavor
- Bread — the CI/CD pipeline of the kitchen (ferment, proof, bake, validate)
- Nutrition — fuel for the brain that does the hacking

**The standard:** You must be able to feed yourself and two others a nutritionally complete, delicious meal from scratch in 45 minutes, with only a knife, a pan, and a heat source. No recipes. No meal kits. No takeout.

## Videography

**Why videography?** Documentation. Communication. Evidence. Storytelling.

A Cybersecuritist who cannot show their work cannot scale their impact.

**What we learn:**
- Camera operation — exposure triangle (aperture, shutter, ISO) as threat model
- Composition — rule of thirds, leading lines, framing as UI/UX
- Lighting — three-point, natural, practical — the exploit payload of visual media
- Audio — the most overlooked vector. Lavalier, shotgun, room treatment. Bad audio = unusable footage.
- Editing — Premiere/DaVinci/CapCut — the compiler for visual code
- Color grading — the post-exploitation phase
- Publishing — YouTube, peer review, internal archive

**The standard:** You must produce a 5-minute technical video explaining a vulnerability you found, with clean audio, proper lighting, clean edits, captions, and a thumbnail that does not lie. Published internally. Peer reviewed.

## Public Speaking

**Not conventional public speaking.** Real hard work.

Conventional public speaking teaches you to smile, make eye contact, use gestures, tell a story. We teach you to explain a buffer overflow to a room containing a CISO, a junior dev, a lawyer, and your grandmother — and have all four understand the impact, the mechanism, and the mitigation.

**What we do:**
- **Technical Translation** — Every session: present a technical topic to a simulated mixed audience. The test: Can the non-technical person explain it back to you?
- **Bug Bounty Debriefs** — "Here's the vulnerability I found in [target]. Here's the impact. Here's the payload. Here's the bypass. Here's what I learned." **[→ Bug Bounty](/docs/spaces/bug-bounty)**
- **Research Presentations** — "Here's the novel technique I developed. Here's the prior art. Here's why it works. Here's the detection." **[→ Research](/docs/spaces/research)**
- **Hardware Demos** — Live soldering, JTAG, fault injection. Things that can go wrong. You explain while your hands work.

**The standard:** You present a 15-minute technical talk to a panel of 3 (1 technical, 1 non-technical, 1 adversarial). The adversarial panelist interrupts with "that's wrong," "that's vague," "so what?" You must handle all three without losing the thread.

## Deep Technical Communication

Writing is thinking made visible. If you cannot write it clearly, you do not understand it clearly.

**What we practice:**
- **Vulnerability Reports** — Clear, reproducible, actionable. The triager should not need to ask questions.
- **Technical Blog Posts** — One per project. The internet is your peer review.
- **Detection Rules** — Sigma/YARA/Snort with false positive analysis and coverage mapping.
- **Post-Mortems** — Blameless. Timeline. Root cause. Action items. Prevention.
- **Documentation** — The kind you wish you had when you started.

**The standard:** Every project produces a teaching session, a technical report, and a detection/mitigation component. No exceptions.

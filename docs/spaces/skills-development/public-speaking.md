# Public Speaking

## Philosophy

Communication is proof of understanding. Hard technical communication under scrutiny.

Conventional public speaking teaches you to smile, make eye contact, use gestures, tell a story. We teach you to explain a buffer overflow to a room containing a CISO, a junior developer, a lawyer, and your grandmother — and have all four understand the impact, the mechanism, and the mitigation.

This is not "soft skills." This is the hardest technical skill there is. If you cannot communicate it, you do not own it.

## Practice

### Technical Translation
Present a technical topic to a simulated mixed audience. The test: Can the non-technical person explain it back to you?

You present a heap overflow. The lawyer summarizes: "So the program asked for a small box, got a big box, and the extra stuff spilled into where the program keeps its secrets?" **Pass.**  
The lawyer summarizes: "You hacked the memory?" **Fail.** You try again.

### Vulnerability Debriefs
"Here's the vulnerability I found. Here's the impact. Here's the payload. Here's the bypass. Here's what I learned."

No vendor names until disclosure. No bounty amounts. Pure technical substance. The room tears apart your logic. You defend or you refine.

### Research Presentations
"Here's the novel technique I developed. Here's the prior art. Here's why it works. Here's the detection."

You cite papers. You show code. You demonstrate live. The adversarial panelist interrupts: "That assumption doesn't hold on Windows." "Your benchmark is single-threaded." "So what?" You handle all three without losing the thread.

### Hardware Demos
Live soldering, JTAG, fault injection. Things that can go wrong. You explain while your hands work.

The oscilloscope shows the glitch. You narrate: "Voltage drop at cycle 3. CPU skips the branch. We're in." The audience sees it. You don't just say it.

## Standard

You present a 15-minute technical talk to a panel of 3 (1 technical, 1 non-technical, 1 adversarial). The adversarial panelist interrupts with "that's wrong," "that's vague," "so what?" You must handle all three without losing the thread.

## Integration

Capstone of the learning model. Theory → Practice → Communication.

You found the bug (Practice). You understand the root cause (Theory). You explain it to the CISO so they fund the fix, to the dev so they implement it correctly, to the lawyer so they understand the liability, to the junior so they learn the pattern (Communication).

Remove communication, and the bug stays unfixed. The knowledge dies with you.

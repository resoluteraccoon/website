# Videography

## Philosophy

Evidence. Education. Presence. Attention to detail. Narrative structure.

A Cybersecuritist who cannot show their work cannot scale their impact.

Video is not "content creation." It is technical documentation in motion. A vulnerability demonstration video is a reproducible proof-of-concept that survives beyond the engagement. A research presentation recorded is knowledge that outlives the meeting. A tutorial on a tool you built is force multiplication — one hour of recording teaches dozens.

The camera does not lie. Bad audio makes footage unusable. Bad lighting hides the exploit. Bad editing obscures the logic. Every technical standard applies: signal-to-noise ratio, bandwidth, latency, compression artifacts, verification.

## Practice

**Technical Videos** — Phone, laptop, free software. One vulnerability explained. One tool demonstrated. One concept taught. Peer reviewed before publish.

**Camera Operation** — Exposure triangle (aperture, shutter, ISO) as threat model. You balance three variables to get the image. Same as balancing stealth, speed, and coverage in a recon operation.

**Composition** — Rule of thirds, leading lines, framing as UI/UX. The viewer's eye follows the path you design. If they miss the payload execution because your terminal was in the corner, you failed.

**Lighting** — Three-point, natural, practical — the exploit payload of visual media. Key light = primary evidence. Fill light = context. Back light = separation from background. No light = no signal.

**Audio** — The most overlooked vector. Lavalier, shotgun, room treatment. Bad audio = unusable footage. A vulnerability demo with humming AC and clipped levels teaches nothing. You would not deploy a detection rule with 90% false positives. Do not publish video with -20 dB noise floor.

**Editing** — Premiere, DaVinci, CapCut — the compiler for visual code. Cut on action. J-cuts for context. L-cuts for continuity. Color grading = post-exploitation. You enhance what matters, suppress what doesn't.

**Publishing** — Internal archive, peer review. Thumbnail that does not lie. Title that describes the technical content. Description with timestamps, links, commands.

## Standard

You must produce a 5-minute technical video explaining a vulnerability you found, with clean audio, proper lighting, clean edits, captions, and a thumbnail that does not lie. Published internally. Peer reviewed.

## Integration

Feeds **Blog** and **Events**. A conference talk recorded becomes a webinar. A hackathon demo becomes a tutorial. A tool walkthrough becomes documentation. Video scales knowledge beyond the room. One recording teaches the next cohort, the remote member, the future you.

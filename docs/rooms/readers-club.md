# Readers Club

## The Standard: One Book Per Month. Deep, Not Wide.

We do not read to say we read. We read to change how we think.

One book per month. Selected by the criteria:

### Selection Criteria

**State-of-the-Art** — The book represents the current frontier of its domain. Not "classic" for classic's sake. The best available thinking *now*.

**Great Pedagogy** — The author teaches. They do not just inform. They hold your hand through the complexity. They anticipate your confusion and resolve it before you feel it.

**"Hold-My-Hand" Technical Depth** — Not pop-sci. Not surface-level. The book goes *there* — into the math, the architecture, the implementation detail. But it brings you with it.

### Three Styles

**Classics** — The books that defined the field. *The Art of Computer Programming*, *Structure and Interpretation of Computer Programs*, *Applied Cryptography*, *TCP/IP Illustrated*. We read them not for history — for the clarity of thought that made them classics.

**Hybrid** — Modern books that bridge theory and practice. *Designing Data-Intensive Applications*, *Operating Systems: Three Easy Pieces*, *Computer Systems: A Programmer's Perspective*, *The Design of the Unix Operating System*.

**Modern** — The current gold standard. *Rust for Rustaceans*, *Zero to Production with Rust*, *Crafting Interpreters*, *Building Git*, *Database Internals*.

## How We Read

### Before the Month
- **Context Briefing** — Why this book? Why now? What gap does it fill in the Refinery?
- **Prerequisite Check** — Every member verifies they have the declared prerequisites. If not, they complete the prerequisite node first.

### During the Month
- **Weekly Sync** — 30 minutes. What confused you? What clicked? What contradicts what you knew?
- **Active Annotation** — Every member maintains a shared annotation layer. Questions. Corrections. Connections to other nodes. Real-world examples.
- **Implementation** — If the book describes an algorithm, you implement it. If it describes a protocol, you capture it. If it describes an architecture, you diagram it.

### End of Month
- **Teaching Session** — One member teaches the book's core thesis to the group in 30 minutes. Non-technical audience test applies.
- **Refinery Integration** — The book's key concepts, diagrams, and insights are injected into the relevant Refinery nodes.
- **Next Selection** — The group proposes and votes on the next book.

## Current Cycle

**Month 1 — Distributed Systems Bible (Martin Kleppmann)**  
The modern bible for distributed systems. Covers storage engines, replication, partitioning, transactions, and stream processing. Every concept grounded in real systems — Kafka, Cassandra, DynamoDB, PostgreSQL. If you build backend systems, this is your textbook.

**Month 2 — Code to Silicon Bridge (Bryant & O'Hallaron)**  
The bridge from code to silicon. Memory hierarchy, linking, exceptions, virtual memory, concurrency. The labs (bomb lab, attack lab, malloc lab) are legendary. You don't just read this — you survive it.

**Month 3 — The Foundation (Donald Knuth)**  
The foundation. Mathematical preliminaries, data structures, algorithms. Not for reading cover-to-cover — for living in. You return to this for decades.

**Month 4 — Database Mechanics (Alex Petrov)**  
LSM trees, B-trees, WALs, compaction, replication. The mechanics beneath every database you use. If you don't understand the storage engine, you don't understand the database.

**Month 5 — OS Pillars (Arpaci-Dusseau)**  
Virtualization, concurrency, persistence. The three pillars. Conversational style, but zero fluff. The homework projects (xv6, threads, fs) cement it.

**Month 6 — Language Implementation (Robert Nystrom)**  
From tokens to bytecode VM. Two implementations: a tree-walking interpreter in Java, a bytecode VM in C. The best way to understand how languages actually run.

## The Standard

You do not "finish" a book. You *master* it.

Mastery means:
- You can derive every key algorithm from first principles.
- You can implement the core data structures without reference.
- You can explain the trade-offs to a non-technical person.
- You have integrated the mental models into your Refinery nodes.

If you cannot do these, you re-read. You re-implement. You re-teach.

There is no "I read it once." There is only "I own it" or "I don't."
